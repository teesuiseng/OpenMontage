Title
Ketohexokinase Protects ER Proteostasis During Nutrient Overload in Hepatocytes

Background
Excessive fructose and fat intake disrupts endoplasmic reticulum function in liver cells, leading to protein misfolding and metabolic dysfunction-associated steatohepatitis.
Ketohexokinase (KHK), the primary fructose-metabolizing enzyme, may have unrecognized roles beyond sugar metabolism in regulating cellular stress responses.

Main Findings

KHK Induction Correlates with Adaptive ER Stress Response
Fructose plus palmitic acid synergistically induced maximal lipid accumulation and robust KHK expression in hepatocytes.
KHK upregulation paralleled activation of IRE1α-XBP1 signaling, a protective branch of the unfolded protein response.
This KHK-IRE1α axis was conserved across human liver biopsies from MASLD patients, primary mouse hepatocytes, and cell culture models.

KHK Loss Shifts ER Stress from Adaptive to Apoptotic
KHK knockdown: reduced adaptive IRE1α-XBP1 signaling and increased protein aggregates and ubiquitination.
KHK knockdown: activated PERK-CHOP pathway, suppressed anti-apoptotic Bcl2, and triggered caspase-8 and caspase-3 cleavage.
Interpretation: KHK deficiency destabilizes proteostasis and redirects hepatocytes toward cell death under nutrient stress.

KHK Supports ER Recovery Independent of Stressor Type
Pharmacologic ER stress (tunicamycin) also induced KHK alongside XBP1 and CHOP.
Chemical chaperone 4-PBA rescued ER stress markers in control cells but failed to suppress CHOP or caspase-3 in KHK-deficient cells.
KHK is required for ER stress resolution regardless of whether stress originates from nutrients or glycosylation inhibition.

Proposed Mechanism
KHK promotes IRE1α-XBP1-mediated adaptive signaling that maintains ER proteostasis and prevents accumulation of misfolded proteins under nutrient overload.
The precise molecular interaction between KHK and UPR components remains unknown and requires further investigation.

Conclusions
KHK functions as both a fructose-metabolizing enzyme and a proteostatic regulator that sustains hepatocyte survival during nutrient stress.
Complete loss of KHK prevents steatosis but compromises ER homeostasis and increases apoptosis.

Significance
This study reveals a dual role for KHK linking fructose metabolism to ER quality control in liver disease progression.
Selective inhibition of KHK enzymatic activity may reduce lipid accumulation while preserving its protective proteostatic function, offering a refined therapeutic strategy for MASLD and MASH.

Limitations
The molecular mechanism by which KHK engages IRE1α-XBP1 signaling is not yet defined.
Therapeutic targeting must balance KHK inhibition with preservation of its non-metabolic protective role to avoid worsening hepatocyte survival.
